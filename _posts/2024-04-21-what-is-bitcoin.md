# What is Bitcoin?**

At first glance, the word “Bitcoin” appears to be a combination of “Bit,” representing binary data (0 and 1), and “coin,” meaning money. In that sense, it may seem like nothing more than digital currency. However, digital money already exists in games and virtual worlds. So what makes Bitcoin fundamentally different?

The key difference lies in control. Unlike traditional currencies or in-game money, no individual, institution, or government can arbitrarily control the supply of Bitcoin. It is issued according to a predefined set of rules and algorithms, making its monetary policy transparent and predictable.

In the traditional financial system, increases in money supply often lead to a decrease in purchasing power over time. This is largely because centralized authorities have the ability to expand the supply of currency when needed. Bitcoin, by contrast, removes this central control and introduces a system where supply is governed by mathematical rules rather than human decisions.

How is such a system even possible?

The answer lies in blockchain technology. A blockchain is a structure where transaction records, known as “blocks,” are linked together in chronological order. These records are not stored in a single location but are distributed across a global network of participants.

Because of this decentralized design, altering past records is extremely difficult. Changing transaction data would require gaining control over the majority of the network’s computational power and rewriting the history of the blockchain—an endeavor that is practically infeasible under normal conditions.

In summary, Bitcoin is a digital currency that operates without central authority, relying instead on a distributed network and cryptographic principles to maintain trust. Its supply is governed by fixed rules, making it fundamentally different from traditional forms of money.
